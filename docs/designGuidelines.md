1. Focus on sustainable progress
The central experience should answer three questions:
What should I do today?
What progress have I made?
What is the smallest useful next step?
Streaks, points, and rewards should support these questions. They should never become more important than the habits themselves.
2. Keep the daily flow extremely short
A strong primary loop would be:
Open app → See today’s habits → Complete or adjust → Receive feedback → Leave
Most daily interactions should take seconds. Reflection, marketplace browsing, and detailed analytics can live in secondary flows.
3. Encourage without becoming childish
Use warm colors, expressive illustrations, friendly motion, and conversational language, but avoid making the interface feel designed only for small children. Teenagers usually respond better to something playful yet mature.
Messages should be specific and believable:
“You showed up three times this week.”
“Missing one day doesn’t erase your progress.”
“Want to make this habit easier tomorrow?”
Avoid generic praise after every interaction.
4. Design for recovery, not perfect streaks
A streak-only model can make one missed day feel catastrophic. Combine streaks with broader measures such as weekly consistency, total repetitions, personal bests, and recovery after missed days.
Never use red warning states, lost currency, or accusatory notifications for ordinary setbacks.
5. Maintain visible commitments without clutter
Today’s habits should be the default focus. Older, paused, or frequently missed habits can appear in a separate commitment review rather than crowding the home screen.
The system should periodically ask whether a habit should be continued, simplified, rescheduled, or intentionally retired. Ending an unsuitable habit should feel like a considered decision, not failure.
6. Build one consistent flow pattern
Most flows should follow the same structure:
Clear goal → One decision per screen → Immediate confirmation → Optional next step
Use consistent components for habit cards, progress indicators, rewards, reminders, and confirmations. Primary actions should remain in predictable locations, with destructive or irreversible actions clearly separated.
7. Make the marketplace transparent and safe
Clearly explain how currency is earned and what each reward costs. Avoid casino-like mechanics, artificial urgency, loot boxes, or manipulative countdowns.
Virtual customization is a particularly good starting point because it provides motivation without encouraging spending or unhealthy competition.
