# User journeys 

1. Starting a first habit
A new user chooses a simple goal such as “drink water after waking up” or “read for 10 minutes.” The user decided the frequency of the habit on a montly / daily/ waikly basis. The user has an option to choose a priority with fires, like snapchat. 
The habit enters a short[1] period in which the app celebrates consistency without demanding perfection. This establishes an early win and teaches the user how habit-building works.
2. Completing the daily routine
The user opens the app and sees a short, **prioritized** list of today’s habits. Each habit can be completed with one tap, postponed, or marked as partially completed.[2]
Completion produces lightweight feedback: progress, points, a friendly message, and perhaps a small animation. The goal is to make the daily check-in satisfying without turning it into a lengthy administrative task.
3. Recovering after missing a habit
When a user misses a habit, the app avoids punishment or guilt-inducing language. Instead, it asks what happened through simple options such as “forgot,” “too difficult,” or “didn’t have time.” For each missed habit, the user can insert a reason "why he missed the habit". After adding the reasons he can review on the calendar why he didn't do the habit on the given day. [3]
Previous commitments remain visible, but they are framed as useful information—not failure.
4. Earning and spending rewards
Completing habits earns points or virtual currency. The user can spend these in a marketplace on avatar items, theme, real items. 
Rewards should reinforce healthy consistency rather than endless engagement. Rare or expensive rewards can represent long-term progress, while smaller rewards provide regular motivation.
5. Reviewing progress and adding another habit
After maintaining a habit for a meaningful period, the user receives a weekly reflection showing consistency, improvements, difficult days, and encouraging observations.
The app may suggest adding one new habit, but only when the current routine appears manageable. The user can accept, postpone, or modify the suggestion. This supports incremental lifestyle change instead of filling the dashboard with too many commitments.
